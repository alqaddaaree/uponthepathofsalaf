ʿIlm al-Kalām

By Ash-Shaykha Umm ʿAbdillah Al-Wādiʿiyyah حفظها الله

She said:

I asked my father رحمه الله: “What is ʿIlm al-Kalām?”
He replied: ʿIlm al-Kalām is a set of methods and rules by which they claim to arrive at knowing the ‘aqīdah.

Then he رحمه الله clarified that ʿIlm al-Kalām is blameworthy, and he said:

Ash-Shāfiʿī رحمه الله said:

“My view and my stance regarding the people of Kalām is that they should be beaten with palm branches, then made to ride on camels, and paraded among the tribes and clans, and it should be announced over them: This is the punishment of the one who abandoned the Book and the Sunnah and turned to ʿIlm al-Kalām.”

(Reported by Abū Nuʿaym Ahmad ibn ʿAbdullah in Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ 9/104)

The great scholars of Kalām themselves admitted that ʿIlm al-Kalām leads to confusion and results in doubts.

Muhammad ibn 'Umar ar-Rāzī said:

نِهَايَةُ إِقْدَامِ الْعُقُولِ عِقَالُ *** وَأَكْثَرُ سَعْيِ الْعَالَمِينَ ضَلَالُ
وَأَرْوَاحُنَا فِي وَحْشَةٍ مِّنْ جُسُومِنَا *** وَحَاصِلُ دُنْيَانَا أَذًى وَوَبَالُ
وَلَمْ نَسْتَفِدْ مِنْ بَحْثِنَا طُولَ عُمْرِنَا *** سِوَى أَنْ جَمَعْنَا فِيهِ قِيلَ وَقَالُوا

"The end result of advancing the intellect is restriction. And the majority of the efforts of mankind lead to misguidance. Our souls remain estranged from our bodies. And all we gained from this world is harm and burden. We did not gain from our lifelong research except compiling “so-and-so said” and “they said.”"

Then he said:

“I have reflected upon the paths of the theologians and the methods of the philosophers, and I did not find them to cure any sick person, nor to quench any thirsty one.”

It is said that Abū Ḥāmid al-Ghazālī and Muhammad ibn 'Umar ar-Rāzī repented, but even if that was the case, their poisonous books remained.

Ash-Shahrastānī said:

لَعَمْرِي لَقَدْ طُفْتُ الْمَعَاهِدَ كُلَّهَا *** وَسَيَّرْتُ طَرْفِي بَيْنَ تِلْكَ الْمَعَالِمِ
فَلَمْ أَرَ إِلَّا وَاضِعًا كَفَّ حَائِرٍ *** عَلَى ذَقَنٍ أَوْ قَارِعًا سِنَّ نَادِمٍ

"By my life, I have wandered through all the places of knowledge, and surveyed all of their landmarks, and I saw none except one placing his hand on his chin in bewilderment, or striking his teeth in regret."

Imām aṣ-Ṣanʿānī responded:

لَعَلَّكَ أَهْمَلْتَ الطَّوَافَ بِمَعْهَدِ *** الرَّسُولِ وَمَنْ لَاقَاهُ مِنْ كُلِّ عَالِمِ
فَمَا حَارَ مَنْ يُهْدَى بِهَدْيِ مُحَمَّدٍ *** وَلَسْتَ تَرَاهُ قَارِعًا سَنَّ نَادِمِ

"Perhaps you neglected to make rounds in the institution of the Messenger, and those scholars who met him and received from him. For no one is ever confused who is guided by the guidance of Muhammad, and you will never see him striking his teeth in regret."

End of Shaykh Muqbil's words.

On Madhhab-following (Taqlīd of a school of thought):

Know, O woman who calls to Allah and teaches (may Allah grant you success), that adhering to a madhhab (school of thought) has no basis in the Sharīʿah. The matter is about following the Qur’an and the Sunnah. These madhāhib came later. Imām al-Bukhārī did not belong to any of the madhāhib, nor did Imām Muslim, nor others from the earlier generations. There was no affiliation to a person. They did not ascribe themselves to Abū Bakr al-Ṣiddīq, nor to ʿUmar, nor to ʿUthmān, nor to ʿAlī. Thus, madhhab-following is something Allah has not sent down any authority for.

And praise be to Allah who has made us see the truth and made madhhabism and partisanship hateful to us.

Indeed, among the blind followers of madhhabs are those who even reject clear evidence. It is said to them: The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said such and such. They reply: But my madhhab is Mālikī, or Shāfiʿī… Did Allah command us to follow the Mālikī madhhab, or the Shāfiʿī, or the Ḥanafī, or the Ḥanbalī? And they reject evidence simply because their madhhab is different.

But Allah the Exalted says:

“Follow what has been sent down to you from your Lord, and do not follow other guardians besides Him. Little do you remember.”
(Sūrat al-Aʿrāf 7:3)

And He says:

"So let those beware who oppose his command, lest a trial befall them or a painful punishment befall them.”
(Sūrat al-Nūr 24:63)

And praise be to Allah who has saved us from the trial of partisanships.

And my father (may Allah have mercy on him) said in response to a question (Fatāwā al-Ḥaramayn, no. 490):

“These madhhabs are a bidʿah with which Allah tested Islām. And I know that many of the doctors, and many of the wearers of cloaks, and many of those with turbans do not like my statement. But if I please my Lord, I do not care about their anger.”

Therefore, it is upon the woman who calls to Allah and teaches to be keen on attaching the students and those she calls to the evidences, and never to shift away from them to the statement of so-and-so or the madhhab of so-and-so—while maintaining respect and reverence for the scholars.

– Shaykha Umm ʿAbdillāh al-Wādiʿiyyah (may Allah preserve her)

بـسمِ اللّٰـه الرَّحمـٰن الرَّحـيم

The majority of the scholars hold that depicting images of beings with souls is prohibited absolutely.

Among them is Imām Aḥmad, based on the generality of the evidences.

Shaykh Ibn Bāz has a treatise titled “The Prohibition of Depicting Beings with Souls.”

Shaykh Ḥammūd al-Tuwayjirī has a treatise titled “Proclaiming Denunciation Against Those Infatuated with Photography” — he has passed away, may Allah have mercy on him. It is a very valuable treatise.

Shaykh Ibn Bāz wrote the introduction to this treatise and mentioned therein that the one who differentiated between photographic images and sculpted images — in other words, between images that have a shadow and those that do not — committed a grave error.

Because the authentic aḥādīth encompass both types, as Shaykh Ibn Bāz رحمه الله states.

This treatise was introduced by Shaykh Ibn Bāz, and he urged that it be read from beginning to end and that its meaning be understood.

Then the criterion is the evidence, and everyone’s statement is accepted or rejected except that of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

On this issue there is a large body of evidence proving its prohibition.

Al-Bukhārī (5963) and Muslim (2110) narrated from Ibn ʿAbbās رضي الله عنهما that he said:

I heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say:
“Whoever makes an image in this world will be commanded to breathe life into it on the Day of Resurrection, and he will not be able to do so.”


The word “image” is indefinite in the context of a conditional clause, which indicates generality — whether one depicts an animal or a human, whether it has a shadow or does not.

The one who makes images is imitating the Lord of the Worlds.

Al-Bukhārī (5953) and Muslim (2111) narrated from Abū Hurayrah رضي الله عنه that he heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say:

“And who is more unjust than one who goes to create like My creation? Let them create a grain, or let them create an atom.”

Muslim (969) narrated in his Ṣaḥīḥ from Abū al-Hayyāj al-Asadī who said: ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib رضي الله عنه said to me: “Shall I not send you on the same mission that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ sent me on? That you do not leave any statue without obliterating it, and no raised grave without leveling it.”

In another narration of Muslim: “Nor any image except that you erase it.”

Al-Tirmidhī (2574) narrated from Abū Hurayrah رضي الله عنه that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

“A neck will emerge from the Fire on the Day of Resurrection, having two eyes with which it sees, two ears with which it hears, and a tongue with which it speaks. It will say: ‘I have been entrusted with three: every tyrannical obstinate one, everyone who calls upon another deity besides Allah, and the image-makers.’”

The ḥadīth is authentic.

The evidences are general, whether the image is of an animal or a human, whether it has a shadow or does not, whether it is three-dimensional or not.

As for that which has a shadow, al-Nawawī said in Sharḥ Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim:
“They unanimously agreed upon the prohibition of that which has a shadow and the obligation of changing it.”

As for that which does not have a shadow, such as images in cars, on walls, or on clothing, then it is prohibited according to what the evidences necessitate.

Al-Nawawī attributed the prohibition in Sharḥ Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim to the majority of the scholars, and this is the view of Aḥmad.

Al-Nawawī رحمه الله said in Sharḥ Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim:
“There is no difference in all of this — meaning in the prohibition — between what has a shadow and what does not have a shadow. This is a summary of our madhhab on the issue, and with its meaning spoke the vast majority of scholars among the Companions, the Tābiʿīn, and those after them. It is the madhhab of al-Thawrī, Mālik, Abū Ḥanīfah, and others.”

When it becomes clear that the majority are upon the prohibition of depicting beings with souls, whether they have a shadow or not:
16:30
As for those who say that what does not have a shadow is permissible, they are refuted by the general evidences and by specific evidences such as the ḥadīth of the cushion (namraqah).

Al-Bukhārī (5181) and Muslim (2107) narrated from ʿĀʾishah رضي الله عنها, the wife of the Prophet ﷺ, that she said: She informed him that she bought a cushion containing images. When the Messenger of Allah ﷺ saw it, he stood at the door and did not enter. I recognized displeasure on his face, so I said: “O Messenger of Allah, I repent to Allah and to His Messenger. What sin have I committed?” He said: “What is this cushion?” She said: “I bought it for you so that you may sit on it and recline upon it.” The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

“Indeed, the makers of these images will be punished on the Day of Resurrection, and it will be said to them: ‘Give life to what you have created.’”

And he said:

“Indeed, the house in which there are images, the angels do not enter it.”

Al-Nawawī said in Sharḥ Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim:

“Some of the Salaf said: Prohibition applies only to what has a shadow, and there is no harm in images that have no shadow. This is a false madhhab, for the curtain in which the Prophet ﷺ denounced the image is unquestionably blameworthy, and its image had no shadow — along with the rest of the unrestricted aḥādīth concerning every image.”

I say:

Imām Aḥmad also held the view of prohibition. Ibn Qudāmah said in al-Mughnī (7/285):

It was said to Abū ʿAbdillāh: “A man rents a house in which there are images — do you think he should scrape them off?” He said: “Yes.”

Al-Marwadhī said: I said to Abū ʿAbdillāh: “I entered a bathhouse and saw an image — do you think I should remove the head?” He said: “Yes.”

This indicates that it was an image without a shadow.

And Allah knows best.

— By Shaykha Umm ʿAbdillāh al-Wādiʿiyyah, may Allah preserve her

What is the ruling on using the heart symbol (❤)?

In the Name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate.

There is a fatwa by the Permanent Committee (24/92), headed by Shaykh Ibn Bāz رحمه الله and Shaykh Ṣāliḥ al-Fawzān حفظه الله, in response to a question that was presented to them along with other issues. The fatwa stated:

That it involves imitation of the people of immorality, who take such symbols as an indication of their love and forbidden passion for others, becoming absorbed in it without regard for the ruling of the pure Sharīʿah concerning it. End quote (paraphrased).

After the ruling has been established that it is imitation of the people of immorality, the ḥadīth of the Prophet ﷺ is well known:
“Whoever imitates a people is one of them.”

My advice to myself and to everyone who reads this message is to refer, in our doubts and issues, back to the firmly grounded scholars, so that we may be upon light and insight in the matters of our religion. For Allah the Mighty and Majestic says:

“And when there comes to them a matter of security or fear, they spread it. But if they had referred it to the Messenger and to those in authority among them, then those who are able to draw correct conclusions from it would have known it.”
(Qur’an 4:83)

Seeking Refuge with Allah from Tribulations (Fitnah)

On the authority of Zayd ibn Thābit رضي الله عنه from the Prophet ﷺ — in the end of the ḥadīth he said:

“Seek refuge with Allah from tribulations, those that are apparent and those that are hidden.”
They said: We seek refuge with Allah from tribulations, those that are apparent and those that are hidden.
He said: “Seek refuge with Allah from the tribulation of the Dajjāl.”
They said: We seek refuge with Allah from the tribulation of the Dajjāl.
رواه مسلم (2867)


this ḥadīth is the command to seek refuge from tribulations, both apparent and hidden.

“What is apparent and what is hidden” means: what is done openly and what is concealed. It is also said: what occurs outwardly upon a person, and what exists in the heart such as shirk (polytheism), showing off (riyā’), envy, and other blameworthy inner thoughts.
(Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 1/209)

This supplication is among the greatest of duʿā’ for seeking refuge from tribulations and their causes.
It includes asking for steadfastness, and it is general—covering all forms of tribulations.
Tribulations are many, and the later the time, the more they increase—becoming numerous, like pieces of a dark night, surging like the waves of the sea.
Among these tribulations are:

• The tribulation of killing and fighting

Al-Bukhārī (447) narrates from Abū Saʿīd al-Khudrī رضي الله عنه:

We used to carry bricks one by one, while ʿAmmār carried two at a time. The Prophet ﷺ saw him, brushed the dust off him and said:
“Woe to ʿAmmār! He will be killed by the transgressing group. He calls them to Paradise, and they call him to the Fire.”
Abū Saʿīd said: ʿAmmār used to say: “I seek refuge with Allah from tribulations.”


• The tribulation of the world and its desires
• The tribulation of illnesses and accidents
• The tribulation of losing loved ones and things dear to the soul
• The tribulation of children
• The tribulation of poverty and wealth
• The tribulation of people with one another

Allah سبحانه وتعالى says:

(And We have made some of you [people] as trial for others - will you have patience?)

The tribulation of people with one another is many: A father may be tested through his child;
a man through his wife;
a woman through her husband;
one may be tested through a neighbor;
or through a companion whose bad manners affect him—as it is said: a companion drags you with him.
Or he may betray him despite good assumptions.
A ruler may also be tested, as his subjects overpower him, show him no respect, and do not recognize his rights over them.

• Division and اختلاف (disagreement)

This is among the greatest types of tribulations.
No one rejoices in tribulations except one in whom there is no khayr.

Rushing toward division and stirring it up indicates corruption of the inner self and absolute ignorance.
So beware of that, hold firmly to your religion, and be keen on uniting the word—this is from taqwā and from our aqidah.

«And when you have completed the prayer, remember Allāh standing, sitting, or [lying] on your sides..» {Sūrat an-Nisāʾ : 103}

ليس كل العلم ينشر | Not every knowledge should be spread

When we share knowledge (of our dīn) we must look at the harms and benefits that will come about—and this requires more knowledge.

Some masāʾil should be left from speaking about to the public. It is from ʿilm that you look to who you are speaking and addressing.

As an example, praying in shoes is from the Sunnah. However, mentioning this to the general public without ḥikmah may lead to harm. People may begin praying in the masjid with dirty shoes, which could cause others to be repelled from coming to the masjid and say, “They claim to follow the Sunnah, yet they dirty the house of Allāh.”

There is a genuine concern when it comes to spreading knowledge and fear of concealing it [Sūrah al-Baqarah:159]; but the ʿulemāʾ placed a criterion on spreading knowledge—between not spreading every bit of it and not withholding all of it.

In Islām, prevention of corruption is given precedence over bringing benefit to prevent corruption being spread out by way of this (even if there is benefit in sharing it).

Ash-Shāṭibī رحمه الله said: “Not every knowledge should be spread/broadcasted. Even if it is true.”

He also added that Imām Mālik mentioned that he has many aḥadīth which he did not speak about nor did he relay it to anyone else—this is because he disliked any speech with no action that comes from it.
“وكان يكره الكلام فيما ليس تحته عمل"
[الموافقات ١٧١:٥]

The criterion the ʿulemāʾ say to use is to present the issue to the Shari’ah. If it is sound according to its scale, then look at its context regarding the state and era of the people—this requires ʿilm and ḥikmah; wisdom which can only be obtained by experience.

There is also the issue of how the knowledge is shared—the way in which it is conveyed; which is another discussion.

Are you in doubt about a certain matter..?

This religion is not based upon doubts. We go upon what is certain.

As our Messenger ﷺ told us:
"دَعْ ما يُريبُك إلى ما لا يُريبُك"
'Leave that which makes you doubt for that which does not make you doubt.'
[Sunan an-Nasa'i 5711]

As an added benefit: hasten to do that which is best!

The way we ask questions is wrong—“is this permissible..?”

Unfortunately, today the only thing that many of us are concerned with is permissibility.

The Ṣaḥābah didn’t do certain things out of fear of falling into the ḥarām, even though they were totally permissible.

For example: it’s permissible to go to the store; you went and there was a male seller. He cracks a joke. You try to hold back your laugh, but you couldn’t. You end up laughing. Your voice rises. You just fell into the ḥarām.

This is not to say that you should not go to the store, however not every permitted deed do we need to do.

Thus, we be careful when we embark upon actions that are permissible.

The Prophet ﷺ used to encourage us to do that which is better, that which is more virtuous.

So we must differentiate between that which is permitted and that which is better. Just because
something is permissible does not mean it is encouraged.

We must, however, have a balanced understanding — we do not beat somebody down because they didn’t necessarily do what’s best; nor do we stick to what’s permitted and not race to do what’s best, and say “the main thing is that’s it’s permitted.”

No—this is not what the Companions رضي الله عنهم were upon, and they did not reach the level of acceptance with Allāh ﷻ by following everything that was merely permitted. In other words, they did not do the bare minimum, but in fact raced to do that which is higher and more praiseworthy.

And the believer follows in the footsteps of the salaf.

Many of us often look at what we’re missing, and it makes us feel lacking — ungrateful. We remind ourselves—and one another—not to focus on what we lack, but to look at what we have. Yet how many of us truly live by this advice? Very few.

﴿وَقَلِيلٌ مِّنْ عِبَادِيَ الشَّكُورُ﴾

﴿إِنَّ الْإِنسَانَ لِرَبِّهِ لَكَنُودٌ﴾
“Indeed, man is truly ungrateful to his Lord.” [al-ʿĀdiyāt: 6]

We forget even the most obvious of blessings — health, sight, sanity, emotions, literacy — and so much more endless blessings, each of them carrying more blessings within it. The greatest of them being that Allah ﷻ guided you to Islam.

Even more than that, He guided you to Ahlus Sunnah, to Ahlul Hadith, to the manhaj of the Salaf.

How can you allow yourself to overlook all this faḍl Allah bestowed upon you? How can sadness dominate your heart when you are surrounded by so many blessings—especially the greatest of them all?

Allah has not given anyone anything greater than Islam.

The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said:

«عَلَيْكُمْ بِسُنَّتِي وَسُنَّةِ الْخُلَفَاءِ الرَّاشِدِينَ الْمَهْدِيِّينَ، عَضُّوا عَلَيْهَا بِالنَّوَاجِذِ»
“Hold firmly to my Sunnah and the Sunnah of the rightly guided caliphs, bite onto it with your molar teeth" [At-Tirmidhi 2676]

But how can we cling to something we do not value? How can we hold firmly to that which we belittle or fail to recognize as a blessing? How can one truly hold onto it when they are constantly fixated on worldly matters — while overlooking this faḍl that, had Allah withheld it, they would have been among the disbelievers? And that—being cursed by Allah and cast into the Hellfire—is a calamity greater than any worldly loss. There is nothing worse than that.

Thank Allah for what He has granted you, and hold onto it tightly, just as the Prophet ﷺ commanded you.

﴿وَكَانَ فَضْلُ اللَّهِ عَلَيْكَ عَظِيمًا﴾
"and ever has the favour of Allah upon you been great" [An-Nisāʾ:113]

Allah chose you—out of millions, billions, trillions of people from the beginning of time until its end—to guide you to the truth and make you among His servants.

What blessing could be greater than that?

Hold onto it firmly, because no one is guaranteed steadfastness until death. You are not promised that you will wake up tomorrow upon guidance.

The Prophet ﷺ warned us of trials so severe that a man may wake up a believer and go to sleep a disbeliever—or go to sleep a believer and wake up a disbeliever.

So ask Allah for steadfastness.

﴿وَإِذْ تَأَذَّنَ رَبُّكُمْ لَئِن شَكَرْتُمْ لَأَزِيدَنَّكُمْ ۖ وَلَئِن كَفَرْتُمْ إِنَّ عَذَابِي لَشَدِيدٌ﴾

The only thing that is truly worth sadness is missing out on khayr you're capable of doing.

Reciting the Qurʾān Hastily

It was narrated In “Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī”¹ from Qatādah, he said: Anas was asked: How was the recitation of the Prophet ﷺ? He said: «He would prolong the sounds in his recitation, then he recited “In the name of Allāh, the most Gracious, the most Merciful,” prolonging ‘Bismillāh’, ‘ar-Raḥmān’, and ‘ar-Raḥīm’.»

_

Explanation:
The ḥadīth indicates that the Prophet's ﷺ recitation was characterized by elongation (madd).

Ibn al-Mulaqqin said: "His Lord—Glorified and Exalted be He—commanded him to recite with careful recitation [tartīl] and to recite it deliberately, and not to move his tongue hastily with it, so he complied to his Lord's command and would recite it at a measured pace, to show his Ummah how they should recite and how they should reflect upon the Qurʾān and understand it."²

That was how the recitation of the Prophet ﷺ was—both within the prayer and outside of it—and it must be taken as a model to follow.

Ibn ʿAbbās –may Allāh be pleased with them– said: «To recite Sūrat al-Baqarah and āl-ʿImrān with a measured pace and reflection is more beloved to me than reciting the entire Qur’ān hastily."³

What does ḥasan gharīb mean?

Shaykh al-Albānī رحمه الله said:

Imām —or al-Ḥāfiẓ— at-Tirmidhī رحمه الله indicates that every chain of narration for which he says “ḥasan gharīb” means that one of its narrators has some slight deficiency in precision and retention of the ḥadīth.

Thus, the ḥadīth is ḥasan in itself; it is not ṣaḥīḥ—because a ṣaḥīḥ ḥadīth requires the narrator to be trustworthy, precise, and strong in memorization—as for a ḥasan ḥadīth, the narrator’s precision and memorization are lesser than that of a ṣaḥīḥ narrator.

So when he says “ḥasan gharīb,” he means that the chain is ḥasan in itself, and that one of its narrators has a slight weakness in memorization.

This is what is intended by at-Tirmidhī’s terminology.

The Virtues of Sending Salutations on the Prophet ﷺ

Ubayy ibn Kaʿb reported: I said, “O Messenger of Allah, I frequently send salutations upon you. How much of my prayers (duʿāʾ) should I assign for making supplications for you?” The Prophet ﷺ said, “As you wish (whatever portion you desire).” I said, “A fourth?” The Prophet ﷺ said, “As you wish, (whatever portion you desire Ubayy) but if you were to add to this, this is better for you.” I said, “A half?” The Prophet ﷺ said, “As you wish, (whatever portion you desire Ubayy) but if you were to add to this, this is better for you.” I said, “Two-thirds?” The Prophet ﷺ said, “As you wish, (whatever portion you desire Ubayy) but if you were to add to this, this is better for you.” I said, “Should I say all of my prayers for blessings upon you?” The Prophet ﷺ said, “If so, your worries will be resolved, and your sins will be forgiven.”

[Sunan al-Tirmidhī 2457]


So the entire time—in the times that you make duʿāʾ—instead of making duʿāʾ for yourself, or your family members or anything of that nature, you are turning that entire time into time in which you are sending ṣalāt upon the Messenger ﷺ.

If this is the case, you will be forgiven of your sins and whatever you have of worries will be sufficed. Subḥanallāh, only by sending salutations upon the Prophet ﷺ — the one sent as an Imām and a leader for us, the one sent to remove us from darkness into the light — repeating this in the time of your duʿāʾ, and giving your entire time of duʿāʾ for yourself to him ﷺ you will be sufficed of your worries.

How many worries do we have?
We know within the depths of ourselves there are things that bother us: family troubles, financial issues, personal struggles, illnesses.

By designating your entire time of supplication to the Prophet ﷺ, then you find Allāh ﷻ suffices you of your worries. Not only that, but you are forgiven of your sins!

And how in need are we to be forgiven of our sins, not just in this period (the blessed month of Ramaḍān), but throughout the year.

When Does Deficiency Become Perfection?

بسم الله الرحمـٰن الرحيم

All praise is due to Allāh, and prayers and peace be upon His Prophet and Chosen One.

Al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn Rajab — may Allāh have mercy on him — said:
Everything that people consider deficient in this worldly life—when it becomes connected to obedience to Allāh and His pleasure—is, in reality, true perfection.


• The odor from the mouths of those who fast for His sake is more pleasant to Him than the fragrance of musk.
• The bareness of those in iḥrām when visiting His House is more beautiful than fine garments.
• The brokenness of the humble before His greatness is true restoration.
• The humility of those who fear His might is true honour.
• The striving of souls to be killed in His path is true life.
• The hunger of those who fast for His sake is true fullness.
• Their thirst in seeking His pleasure is true quenching.
• The fatigue of the diligent in serving Him is true rest.

The humiliation of a young man in love is a virtue, and his submission to his Beloved is an honour.

Today a breeze from the breezes of nearness has blown upon the hearts. The broker of admonitions has come seeking reconciliation with those who were abandoned.

Glad tidings have reached those who were cut off—that they will be reconnected, those who sinned—that they will be forgiven, and those deserving the Fire—that they will be freed.

Knowing When Laylat al-Qadr Occurs

Questioner: How does a Muslim know that he has encountered Laylat al-Qadr while dilgently seeking it during the nights that are narrated from the Prophet ﷺ?

Al-ʾImām al-ʾAlbānī -may Allāh have mercy on him-: That is a sentimental matter felt by everyone whom Allāh—Blessed and Exalted is He—has favored to witness Laylat al-Qadr. This is because on this night the person turns to worshiping Allāh—Mighty and Majestic is He—to His remembrance, and to prayer. Allāh—Mighty and Majestic—then reveals to some of His servants a feeling that not even the righteous experience at other times, this feeling is what can be relied upon.

ʿĀʾishah -may Allāh be pleased with her- conveyed to the Messenger ﷺ a question that indicates whether a person can sense witnessing Laylat al-Qadr.

She asked the Prophet ﷺ: "O Messenger of Allāh, if I know which night is Laylat al-Qadr, what should I say during it?" He said: «Say: O Allāh, You are Pardoning and love pardon, so pardon me.»


In this ḥadīth, there are two benefits:

1- A Muslim can have a personal, subjective feeling of encountering Laylat al-Qadr.
2- If he feels that, the best supplication to make is this one: "O Allāh, You are Pardoning and love pardon, so pardon me."

On this occasion, it is mentioned this book of ours, "At-Targhīb," in some lessons, that the best thing a person can ask his Lord—Blessed and Exalted is He—is for pardon and well-being (ʿāfiyah) in this world and the Hereafter.

Yes, there are some signs and physical indicators of Laylat al-Qadr, but not everyone who witnesses and knows Laylat al-Qadr can see these. That is because some of these signs relate to the general external atmosphere. For example, the night might be neither hot nor cold, but rather temperate. However, a person might be in an environment where he cannot perceive the natural climate of the area.

There is also a sign, but it appears after Laylat al-Qadr in the morning of that night at sunrise. The Prophet ﷺ informed us that the sun rises on the morning of Laylat al-Qadr appearing like a moon, without rays. That is how the sun rises on the morning of Laylat al-Qadr, and this has been observed by some righteous people who cared about seeing and noticing it on many nights of Laylat al-Qadr.

What's important regarding the devoted worshipper is not to cling to such physical phenomena, because these phenomena are general, meaning they pertain to the nature of the climate. Not everyone who lives in that climate shares in witnessing Laylat al-Qadr, meaning: in having that spiritual clarity at one of the moments during that blessed night, such that Allāh—Mighty and Majestic—bestows upon them His mercy and grace, inspiring one and causing him to supplicate with what has preceded along with other supplications.

Verily physical signs are signs, but they do not indicate that everyone who witnesses or experiences them has attained Laylat al-Qadr—and this is the reality. However, the aspect that should be focused on is the spiritual clarity within oneself and the feeling of witnessing Laylat al-Qadr, and turning to Allāh with prescribed supplications.

This is the aspect we should focus on and be concerned with, hoping that Allāh—Mighty and Majestic—may bestow it upon us by His grace.

Informing the People of ʾĪmān of the Detailed Ruling Regarding Sadness Over the Departure of Ramaḍān 🌙

The time of departure has drawn near, and just as we used to see messages of joy and longing, we now see messages of sadness and departure.

People have differed regarding the ruling on feeling sad over its departure. Some said it is not allowed, others said feeling sadness at its departure is like joy at its arrival.

The detailed answer is as follows:

Firstly: The original ruling is to show joy, due to the statement of the Prophet ﷺ
«There are two joys for the fasting person: the joy when he breaks his fast, and the joy of when he meets his Lord.»
Joy at breaking the fast is of two types.

1️⃣ Joy at the time of ifṭār at the end of the daily fast, and this is a recurring joy throughout the month.
2️⃣ Joy at the time of ʿĪd al-Fiṭr at the end of the monthly fast, and this is the final joy attached to breaking the fast. This is what is intended by the ḥadīth, and its meaning also includes the first [type].

Know that people, in their joy, are two types:
🔴 The first type:
A joy that entails hatred of fasting and happiness at being relieved from it. This is nonexistent except with hypocrites.
🟢 The second type:
A joy that does not mean relief from something hated, but rather a transition from one joy to another.

Accordingly, there are two joys:
● The joy of worship.
● The joy of completing worship.
All of this is permissible and legislated without doubt, and it is what is intended in the ḥadīth.

What remains is the summary of this situation: Is it correct to feel sadness over the departure of Ramaḍān?

The answer is from two angles.

The First Angle:
The original ruling is to show joy, based on two evidences:

1️⃣ The statement of Allāh:
"(He wants that you) must complete the same number (of days), and that you must magnify Allāh [i.e. to say Takbīr] for having guided you so that you may be grateful to Him."

This indicates joy from two aspects:
🌴 First: Takbīr
The takbīr at the end of fasting contradicts the display of sadness; rather, it is a symbol of joy, celebration, and gladness.
🌴 Second: Gratitude
Gratitude is not for a calamity, but for a blessing — and a blessing necessitates joy, not sadness.

2️⃣ Mentioned in the ḥadīth:
«There are two joys for the fasting person: the joy when he breaks his fast, and the joy of when he meets his Lord.»
Just as you do not feel sadness at meeting your Lord, likewise you should not feel sadness at breaking your fast as the two joys are paired together.

The Second Angle:
Sadness is an incidental matter that opposes the foundation, and it has two cases.

🚫 First case: That a person feels sad wishing Ramaḍān were longer, believing that this would be better. This sadness is invalid and a misguidance, because it opposes what Allāh has legislated, implying that something else is better than what Allāh decreed, and we seek refuge in Allāh.

✅️ Second case: That a person feels sad due to missing out on good because of his own shortcomings, negligence, or preoccupation. This is permissible, even if it comes late, as it is a form of regret over missing righteous deeds.

However, its permissibility is conditional upon not publicizing or displaying this sadness, because the Sharīʿah commands takbīr and joy. Thus, this is a private state that remains with the individual and should not extend to others.

*It was not from the way of the Salaf to display sadness during the days of ʿĪd at all; rather this contradicts their guidance.*

Tawakkul — What It Truly Is and How It Is Achieved

A question posed to ash-Shaykhah Umm ʿAbdillāh bint ash-Shaykh Muqbil al-Wādi’yyah حفظها الله


The Question:
One of my sisters for the sake of Allāh asks:
Our Lord, Glorious and Exalted, has indeed taught us that whosoever puts his trust in Allāh, then He will suffice him. So how can a person place true reliance upon their Lord in a way that pleases Him, and what are the means that help achieve this noble act of worship?

The Answer:

Understanding the meaning of tawakkul clarifies what is meant by it. It is truly depending upon Allāh, not turning to oneself nor to anyone else besides Him, Glorified and Exalted.

Ibn Rajab, may Allāh have mercy on him, said in Jāmiʿ al-ʿUlūm wa al-Ḥikam (2/497):
“It is the heart's true reliance upon Allāh—Glorified and Exalted—in seeking benefits and warding off harm in all matters of this world and the Hereafter. It is entrusting all affairs to Him and affirming with true faith that no one gives, withholds, harms, or benefits except Him”

Ibn al-Qayyim said in Madārij al-Sālikīn (1/103):

If you ask: What is the meaning of tawakkul and (ʾistiʿānah) seeking assistance?

I say: it is a state of the heart that arises from one’s knowledge of Allāh and belief in His singularity in creation, disposal of affairs, harm, benefit, giving, and withholding. Whatever He wills occurs even if people do not will it, and whatever He does not will does not occur even if people will it. This necessitates reliance upon Him, entrusting matters to Him, finding tranquility in Him, trusting Him, and being certain of His sufficiency for what one relies upon. The heart becomes full of Him, and this does not happen except by His will, whether people will it or not. This resembles the state of a child with its parents regarding what he hopes for and fears, as they are fully capable of fulfilling those for him. Observe how his heart is free from turning to anyone other than his parents and how his concerns are confined to attaining what he intends through them. This is the state of the one who relies upon Allāh.”

Ash-Shaykh Ibn Bāz said in Majmūʿ al-Fatāwā (4/427):

“Tawakkul combines two things:

Firstly: Dependence upon Allāh and belief that He is the Causer of all causes, that His decree is executed, and that He has decreed, recorded, and written all matters, Glorified and Exalted is He.

Secondly: Acting upon the means; for tawakkul does not mean abandoning the means. Rather, it combines acting upon the means while depending on Allāh.” End quote (intended meaning)

From the things that aid dependence on Allāh alone include:

📍 Beneficial knowledge: For it is a radiant light that guides to the straight path.

📍 ʿAqīdah: Whoever has ʿaqīdah (creed) and knowledge of Allāh, His Names and Attributes, His Messengers and His Books, will attain tawakkul which will increase him in strength.

📍 Faith in Divine Decree (Al-Qadr): My father, ash-Shaykh Muqbil, may Allāh The Exalted have mercy on him, mentioned in Al-Jāmiʿ aṣ-Ṣaḥīḥ fī al-Qadr (14) that among the fruits of belief in Divine Decree are tawakkul, certainty, dependence on Allāh, and submission to Him:
“Say: Nothing shall ever happen to us except what Allāh has ordained for us." [At-Tawbah : 51]

📍 Knowing the biographies of the Prophets: For they are examples for those who rely upon Allāh.

📍 Frequent remembrance of Allāh, supplication, and seeking forgiveness: Our Lord says: “Those who believed (in the Oneness of Allāh- Islāmic Monotheism), and whose hearts find rest in the remembrance of Allāh: verily, in the remembrance of Allāh do hearts find rest” [Ar-Raʿd : 28]

📍 Humility: Ibn al-Ḥājj said in Al-Madkhal (3/71):
“When humility settles in the heart, all goodness settles within it: compassion, gentleness, mercy, submissiveness, contentment, tawakkul, good assumptions, intense modesty, good manners, renouncing greed, striving against the self, extending goodness, serenity of the heart, preoccupation away from oneself, hastening to do good, and slowing from evil.”

It is Not Permissible to Follow This Person

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyyah رحمه الله said regarding Allāh’s Statement, Most High:

ٱتَّخَذُوٓا۟ أَحْبَارَهُمْ وَرُهْبَـٰنَهُمْ أَرْبَابًا مِّن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ

“These people who took their rabbis and monks as lords – as they obeyed them in permitting what Allāh prohibited and prohibiting what Allāh permitted – are of two types.

“The first is that they know that they changed Allāh’s Religion, following them in this change and thus believing the permissibility of what Allāh prohibited and prohibition of what Allāh permitted in following their leaders while knowing that they opposed the Religion of the Rusul (Messengers). This is kufr (disbelief), and Allāh and His Rasūl have declared this shirk, even if they do not perform ṣalāh to them (i.e., these rabbis and monks) and prostrate to them. Hence, whoever follows someone else in opposing the Religion while knowing that it is opposition to the Religion and believes in what they say instead of what Allāh and His Rasūl said is a mushrik just like these people.

“The second is that their belief and īmān in [the impermissibility] of prohibiting permitted things and permitting prohibited things is as it should be, however they obeyed them in disobeying Allāh, just as the Muslim does whatever sins he does while believing them to be sins — these people have the ruling of their likes among those who do sins, just as it has been established from the Nabī ﷺ that he said:

إِنَّمَا الطَّاعَةُ فِي المَعْرُوفِ
“ ‘Obedience is only in goodness,’ [al-Bukhārī (6830) and Muslim (1840)].

“Furthermore, if this person who prohibits that which is permissible and permits that which is prohibited is a mujtahid whose intent is following the Rasūl, however the truth was hidden from him at the same time and he feared Allāh as much as he was able, then Allāh does not punish this person for his mistake. In fact, He rewards him for his ijtihād by which he obeyed his Rabb.

“However, whoever knows that this is a mistake regarding what the Rasūl came with and then follows him in his mistake and deviates from the statement of the Rasūl, then this person has a portion of the shirk which Allāh rebuked, especially if he followed his desires in doing so and aided [this position] with his hand and tongue while knowing that this is in opposition to the Rasūl. 🌱 Understanding the Nature of Children

When you understand the nature of children, many things that would normally cause frustration become easier to bear with patience and wisdom.

A young child — from birth to usually around four years old — naturally sees the world as revolving around them, everything belongs to them in their head. They may take things that don’t belong to them or refuse to share, not out of bad intention, rather it's their nature, they haven’t yet developed the concept of ownership or the awareness of others — they'll eventually grow out of it if you teach them well with gentleness.

So instead of reacting with anger, you respond by teaching them with patience and ḥilm (forbearance). They are not disobeying out of bad intention, but lack of understanding.

Similarly, younger children are naturally drawn to what they can see and experience more than what they are told.

If, for example, an eight year old child delays prayer or prefers playing games over ḥifẓ, it's not because they hate worship or reject obedience to Allāh, rather, it is because:

The ghayb (unseen) is still abstract to them, while the dunya is immediate and tangible. So they don't want to "miss out" on the fun while praying or memorising.

And this is something even adults struggle with, let alone children.

So tarbiyah here requires:

• Gradual nurturing of īmān
• Patience over years, not days
• Consistency without harshness

You plant the seed early, but you don’t expect the tree to grow overnight.

This also extends beyond children.

When you understand people's nature, you begin to deal with them with wisdom, not mere reactions.

• A man who understands emotional fluctuations may show more patience and sympathy instead of taking things personally.

• A woman who understands that anger is a human nature Allāh trialed men with especially may respond with more restraint instead of provocatively.

So you deal with people with their level of understanding — not your own.

With that said, this understanding is an excuse for others, but not a justification for ourselves.

You assume the best of your Muslim brothers and sisters, you excuse them, you show mercy.

But you also hold yourself accountable, you strive against the blameworthy traits you were tested with, you don’t hide behind “this is just my nature”.

The Head Is the Image

My sisters in Islam:

Being lenient regarding images and picture-making is extremely dangerous. It is one of the major sins. It involves being cursed, and it prevents angels from entering, which in turn allows devils to take over.

What is the ruling on these images? And what must we avoid?

Images of living beings are prohibited when the head is present, based on the Prophet’s statement ﷺ:
«الصُّورَةُ الرَّأسُ، فَإِذَا قُطِعَ الرَّأسُ فلَا صُورَةَ»
"The image is the head; if the head is cut off, there is no image." [As-Sahihah, 1921]

And his statement ﷺ:
«فَمُرْ بِرَأْسِ التِّمْثَالِ الَّذِي فِي الْبَيْتِ يُقْطَعُ، فَيَصِيرُ كَهَيْئَةِ الشَّجَرَةِ»
"Command that the head of the statue in the house be cut off, so it becomes like the shape of a tree." [Narrated by Abu Dawud, 4158, from Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him - graded 'Sahih' by Sheikh Albani]

The head is well-known. Blurring the eyes or the face does not suffice as an alternative to cutting off the head!

Whether the head is facing forward or backward, and whether the facial features are visible or not—it is still a head. Once the head is removed, we are no longer in violation. The image is (essentially) the head!

As for the remaining body parts of living beings, scholars have differed. The more cautious approach is to avoid them unless there is a need. However, this applies only if the body parts are NOT THOSE OF A WOMAN. It is not permissible to display a woman’s body parts, neither in reality nor in images, based on the Prophet’s statement ﷺ:
«المَرْأَةُ عَوْرَةٌ»
"A woman is ‘awrah (to be covered)." [Narrated by At-Tirmidhi, 1173, from Ibn Mas‘ud, may Allah be pleased with him)]

A woman is commanded to cover herself and conceal her adornment. Her entire body is to be covered. So what about those who display women’s hands decorated with henna? This is a source of fitnah.

And Allah is the One whose help is sought.

How to Achieve Sincerity When Seeking Knowledge

ʾImām Aḥmad -may Allāh have mercy on him- said: «Knowledge is equaled by nothing for the one whose intention is sound.» They asked: "And how does one's intention become sound, O Abā ʿAbdillāh?" He replied: «He intends to remove ignorance from himself and from others.»

If one asks: How is sincerity achieved in seeking knowledge?

We say: Sincerity in seeking knowledge is achieved by intending the following matters:

• The First Matter: Fulfilling the command of Allāh, as Allāh the Exalted has commanded it. He said:
"So know (O Muḥammad ﷺ) that, Lā ilāha illallāh (none has the right to be worshipped but Allāh), and ask forgiveness for your sin..” [Sūrat Muḥammad: 19]
And He—Glorified and Exalted is He—encouraged knowledge. Encouraging something necessitates loving it, being pleased with it, and commanding it.

• The Second Matter: Preserving the Shari'ah of Allāh, as it is achieved through learning, memorization in the chest, and through writing.

• The Third Matter: Protecting the Shariʿah and defending it. For were it not for the scholars, the Shariʿah would not be protected, nor would anyone defend it. We see, for example, Shaykh al-ʾIslām Ibn Taymiyyah and other scholars who faced the people of innovation and exposed the falsehood of their innovations. We can see they obtained abundant good.

• The Fourth Matter: Following the Shariʿah of Muḥammad ﷺ, for you cannot follow his Shariʿah unless you know it.

These are four matters, all of which are encompassed by our statement: Sincerity to Allāh is obligatory in seeking knowledge.

📌 There are 3 levels of forbidding evil (munkar) from the ḥadīth of the Messenger of Allāh ﷺ:
"Whoever amongst you sees an evil, he must change it with his hand; if he is unable to do so, then with his tongue; and if he is unable to do so, then with his heart; and that is the weakest form of ʾImān." Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim (49).


(1) Forbidding evil with the hand.
This is the strongest level, such as: Removing wine, breaking idols as the Prophet ʾIbrāhīm عليه السلام did, as well as with the Prophet ﷺ removing a gold ring from a man's hand.

(2) Forbidding evil with the tongue.
This is done with wisdom and gentleness, unless the situation calls for harshness and severity. If one fears that this may lead to a greater evil, then he should move to the next level. Important Reminder on the Matter of Note-Taking
———•———•———•———•———

In the most recent lesson of al-Mabādī al-Mufīdah by Ustādh Abū Hurayrah Al-Brīṭānī, the issue of note-taking and sharing notes was discussed. Ustādh noted mistakes or things that could be better.

So, how does a person take good notes?

The aṣl is that it is not done digitally, but it is done by pen and paper. If one wishes, he can write them up digitally after writing by hand. This is khair—no problem in this.

One benefit of this: taking notes by writing physically is a means for a person to memorise and remember [the content] very well. As your mind is 100% engaged in what you’re doing, it becomes ingrained. The phone/computer is not the same.

What does a person write?

The aṣl is that he should write every single word the teacher says. Why? Because the teacher says the words he says for a reason and he chooses the words carefully and says it with a purpose.

Every single word is important!

If they are able, they should strive to write every single word.

Of course, if the teacher repeats something to make it clear and allow it to stay in your mind and to make sure it’s understood, this is a chance to catch up what’s missed—you don't need to write it again.

Pay attention to the words. Don’t change words.
This can inadvertently change the meaning.

For example, if the teacher says something has been “narrated from Prophet ﷺ,” and in their notes they say “the Prophet ﷺ said this…” —but this is not what Ustādh has said.

There is a difference between these two things. When the Ustādh said this [the former], he is weakening this narration or indicating it may not be ṣaḥīḥ. However, if one writes the latter in their notes, they are attributing to the teacher something he did not say.

If they [the teacher] say “in my opinion..” or “Allāh knows best, this is the correct position..” and they write “the correct opinion is..”, this is not right. The former wording is indicating the fact that the teacher could be incorrect.

Therefore,
•Pay close attention to wordings used and the intent, especially if it’s being published or shared with other people.
•If a person misses something, then just leave a blank space. If you fill in the gaps [yourself], you may well attribute something to the teacher that they didn’t say. 
•If there’s a recording, then fill in gaps later, if not then compare notes with others. If you try fill in gaps, you may be incorrect.

Strive to write everything a teacher has said!

After the lesson, one can summarise notes in a way that makes it easy to revise.

From the virtues of Khadījah bint Khuwaylid, may Allāh be pleased with her.

بِـسم اللّٰـه الرّحمـٰن الرّحيـم

“She never compelled him to raise his voice” So take Khadījah—may Allāh be pleased with her—as your example, O women!

Abū Hurayrah (may Allāh be pleased with him), narrated: Jibrīl came to the Prophet ﷺ and said: “O Messenger of Allāh! This is Khadījah, she has come to you carrying a vessel containing condiments and food. When she comes to you, convey to her greetings of peace from her Lord and from me, and give her glad tidings of a house in Paradise made of hollowed pearls (qaṣab), wherein there is no clamour (ṣakhab) nor fatigue (naṣab).” Agreed upon.

It is mentioned in Fatḥ al-Bārī (11/134): “Made of qaṣab”: Meaning a hollow pearl, spacious like a lofty palace.

As-Suhaylī said: “The subtle point in the wording “qaṣab” instead of “pearl” is that it corresponds to her having attained the lead (qaṣab al-sabaq) by hastening to faith before others. This is why this connection appears throughout this entire ḥadīth." [End quote]

There is another connection in “qaṣab” as most of its tubes are straight and even—likewise Khadījah had a levelness not found in others. She was keen upon pleasing him ﷺ in every possible way, and nothing ever came from her that angered him, unlike others.

“A house”: Meaning a house in addition to what Allāh prepared for her as the reward of her deeds. Hence he said: “nor fatigue”, meaning she did not experience hardship in attaining it.

As-Suhaylī said: "Mentioning "house" has a subtle meaning, because she was a mistress of a house before the Prophethood, then she became the mistress of a house of ʾIslām alone! As there was no house of ʾIslām on earth at the first day of the Prophet’s ﷺ prophethood except her house—this is a virtue no one else shared."
He added: "Reward is often mentioned using the same expression as the deed, even if the reward is more noble. Hence the ḥadīth says “house” rather than “palace.”" [End quote]

And in mentioning the “house” lies another meaning, reflecting that the Prophet’s household [children] ultimately returned to her.

“Wherein there is no ṣakhab nor naṣab”: Ṣakhab: Arguments and dispute with raised voices. Naṣab: Fatigue.

As-Suhaylī said: “The suitability of negating these two is that when the Prophet ﷺ called her to ʾIslām, she responded willingly—she did not make him raise his voice, nor dispute, nor struggle. Rather, she removed from him all hardship, made him feel at ease from every loneliness, and made every difficulty easy for him. So it was fitting that her dwelling, with which her Lord gave her glad tidings, be described with the qualities corresponding with her actions.” (End of quote, abridged)

———•———•———•———•———

In ‘Umdat al-Qārī (16/281):
“..The mention of “clamour” and “fatigue” is also by way of correspondence; as when he ﷺ called her to faith, she responded quickly and did not require him to raise his voice as a man might if his wife resisted him, nor did she cause him hardship. Rather, she removed every hardship from him, made him feel at ease from every loneliness, eased every difficulty, and relieved burdens with her wealth. Thus her abode was described in a way that corresponds to her state and actions.” [End quote]

———•———•———•———•———

In Tafsīr ibn Kathīr (6/404):
“From the special qualities of Khadījah (may Allāh be pleased with her) is that she never upset him, never angered him, and he never had to take an oath of abstinence from her, nor was there ever any blame or abandonment between them. And this alone is a sufficient virtue and merit.” [End quote]

So may Allāh be pleased with our mother Khadījah and all the Mothers of the Believers, and gather us with them in the highest Paradise. Indeed, my Lord is All-Hearing, All-Knowing.

Not Every Interruption Is An Interruption

So there she sits with her book…finally, after a long day, she finally finds a quiet moment. A page opens, her mind slowly settles, and ideas begin connecting.

But then:

"Ummi…"

A scribb…sorry, a piece of art…needs admiration.
A button that needs "fixing".
A story that goes nowhere needs an ear, right now!
Or if they feel like being honest, then no reason except that they want her near…now the button doesn't need fixing.

She perhaps feels pulled away from something "more valuable" (you heard that right).

Because these moments feel small while they are happening.

A question.
A scribb…sorry, again, a piece of art.
A child asking her to watch something unimportant (how dare you) for the third time that day.


And when things repeat often enough, the nafs slowly begins treating them as background noise instead of something weighty.

And perhaps that is the danger.

That these moments feel so small...that a mother can begin seeing them as obstacles standing between her and what she wanted to finish.

But yā ukhti, childhood does not remain in this form for long.

A child who keeps pulling you away today will not always do so.

And this child is not trying to stand between you and knowledge.

He only wants his mommy.

He does not know what masʾalah you are studying.
He does not understand what benefit made you pause and reflect.
He cannot distinguish between tafsīr, fiqh, and ʿaqīdah.

But he understands something else very clearly.

He understands whether your presence feels gentle…in those moments.
Whether he feels welcomed near you…in those moments.
Whether knowledge made the atmosphere of the home colder…or warmer.

This is something many mothers do not notice: the child is not only watching that you study, he is watching how you treat him while you study.

He sees whether knowledge made you harsher…or gentler.

Whether your book always feels like something that takes you away from him…or something that still leaves room for warmth beside it.

So even these interruptions are part of his education, and your education.

Because before a child loves knowledge itself, he first learns how knowledge feels through the one carrying it.

And that knowledge you're seeking, is just a means to worship Allāh correctly.

Among the most important acts of worship for a woman, is, taking care of her children.

So, letting knowledge seeking take more priority than your child defeats the purpose.

Think of it as overeating. The point is health, not food itself.

Now back again to your child:

Your child feels the atmosphere while those books are present.

Warm? Cold? Harsh? Gentle?

These early feelings settle deep.

Years later, he may not remember the exact books you read.

But he will remember the atmosphere surrounding them.

The woman who kept learning…without making him feel abandoned by her learning.

The woman whose pursuit of knowledge did not erase softness from the home.

So balance.

Do not let your child feel that knowledge stole you from him.

And do not let the light of knowledge disappear from the home entirely.

Let your child grow up seeing that knowledge and mercy were never enemies.

That the same hands which held the book…still held him gently too. 🌿

Thought Process Behind The Rewrite Above

One thing I noticed while rewriting the original piece is that the problem was not the ideas themselves.

The ideas are actually good.

The problem was the movement between them.

Writing quality is not just vocabulary, emotional lines, metaphors, or sentence beauty.

But a huge part of strong writing is structural flow.

Meaning: why does this paragraph come after that one?

The original piece had multiple good ideas:

children interrupting the mother

these moments being temporary

the child loving the mother

motherhood refining mercy

the child later admiring knowledge

balance

But the piece kept changing centers abruptly.

For example, it went from: "the child loves his mother", to: "motherhood refines the woman spiritually".

Then suddenly: "the child later admires knowledge."

Those are not naturally connected yet.

They are individually good ideas, but the transitions are weak.

So instead of adding more lines, I tried identifying the actual spine of the piece.

The stronger center became: The child experiences knowledge through the mother carrying it.

That single idea reorganized the structure.

Now the interruptions themselves become important.

Why?

Because the child is not merely seeing that his mother studies.

He is seeing:

how she reacts while studying

whether knowledge made her gentle or harsh

whether the book emotionally pushes him away

or whether warmth still exists beside it

Now the paragraphs connect naturally.

The interruptions lead into emotional atmosphere.

The atmosphere leads into the child's perception of knowledge.

That perception leads into how he may later love knowledge himself.

Then another issue appeared.

The piece still treated knowledge as though it were separate. Which is the core cause of this whole topic.

So the hierarchy itself needed correction.

Knowledge is a means.

The goal is worshipping Allāh correctly.

And among the worship of a woman is properly caring for her children.

So if knowledge-seeking causes neglect of the child, then the means started damaging the objective itself.

That is why I added the overeating analogy.

Food is good.
Health is the goal.

Likewise:
Knowledge is good.
Correct worship is the goal.

That change strengthened the entire ending because now the conclusion comes from principles.

A better form of writing avoids stacking ideas beside each other instead of making one idea produce the next one naturally.

The reader should feel carried forward gradually, not teleported between paragraphs.

Like a gradient. 🌿

Rather, a Muslim should remember Allah, thank Him for His blessings, humble themselves before Him, and reflect on His favour and kindness toward them.

A sister asks: Is it better for a female seeker of knowledge (Ṭālibat ʿIlm) to marry a seeker of knowledge (Ṭālib ʿIlm)?

Shaykha Umm ʿAbdillāh Al-Wādiʿiyyah حفظها الله says:

🕰️Marriage is a blessing from Allāh the Exalted, and it is the Sunnah of the Prophets and Messengers.

Allāh the Exalted says:

{وَلَقَدْ أَرْسَلْنا رُسُلًا مِنْ قَبْلِكَ وَجَعَلْنا لَهُمْ أَزْواجاً وَذُرِّيَّة} [الرعد: 38].
'And indeed We sent Messengers before you (O Muhammad ﷺ) and made for them wives and offspring.' [ar-Raʿd: 38]

Circumstances vary, and the matter ultimately depends on Allāh's success and provision. Through duʿāʾ and persistent supplication to your Lord, you may attain what you desire.

If a Ṭālibat ʿIlm is blessed with the opportunity to marry a Ṭālib ʿIlm (then it should be one) who possesses good character. Not just any Ṭālib ʿIlm will do!

Rather, he must be a man of good character who considers a woman's feelings, respects her, honors her, and maintains a mutual understanding with her.

Allāh the Exalted says:

{وَالَّذِينَ يَقُولُونَ رَبَّنَا هَبْ لَنَا مِنْ أَزْوَاجِنَا وَذُرِّيَّاتِنَا قُرَّةَ أَعْيُنٍ وَاجْعَلْنَا لِلْمُتَّقِينَ إِمَامًا} [الفرقان:74].
“And those who say: ‘Our Lord, grant us from among our spouses and offspring comfort to our eyes and make us leaders for the righteous.’” [al-Furqān: 74]

Imām as-Saʿdī رحمه الله said in his explanation of this verse:

“Meaning: make them a source of joy and comfort for our eyes. When one reflects upon their condition and qualities, it becomes clear that their aspirations are lofty and their rank is high. Their eyes are not truly comforted until they see their spouses and children obedient to their Lord, possessing knowledge and acting upon it.

This supplication is not only for the righteousness of their spouses and offspring; it is also a supplication for themselves, because its benefit returns to them. That is why they said: ‘Hab lanā’.¹ In fact, their supplication also benefits the Muslims in general, because the righteousness of those mentioned becomes a cause for the righteousness of many others who are connected to them and benefit from them.”
Therefore, if a Ṭālibat ʿIlm finds a Ṭālib ʿIlm who possesses these qualities, then excellent indeed.

If such a person is not available, then what matters is that, along with good character, he is righteous and steadfast upon the religion; that he hates music, is chaste, and stays away from betrayal and ḥarām relationships.

Al-Bukhārī (5090), and Muslim (1466) Narrated from Abū Hurayrah رضي الله عنه that the Prophet ﷺ said:
"A woman is married for four (things): Her wealth, her lineage, her beauty, and her religion. So (be keen to) attain the one with religion, may your hands be rubbed with dust (i.e, "May you prosper")."

My father رحمه الله disapproved of this and said: "What benefit will his knowledge be to her if she does not strive diligently for herself?"

"His Pen Suffices Us"

Alḥamdulillāh, to proceed:

Indeed, the doors of evil through which Ṣhayṭān enters upon people—both the righteous and the wicked among them—are many. Among these is making comparisons between scholars and callers to Allāh. These comparisons cause the one making them to fall into backbiting, which is a major sin. They also lead to the disparagement and contempt of Allāh’s righteous servants, which is arrogance—another major sin.

One form of these comparisons is focusing on what person X lacks compared to person Y. This holds true whether the comparison is made by an idle person afflicted with ignorance who has nothing of value to offer and thus occupies himself with comparisons, or by someone who has been blessed with a specific aspect of calling to Allāh [Daʿwah] but looks down on others out of self-admiration. All of this is an affliction that must be treated and guarded against.

And it has pleased me what some have narrated regarding Shaykh Ibn Bāz:

In al-Masjid al-Ḥarām, Shaykh Ibn Bāz requested Shaykh Bakr ibn ʿAbdullāh Abū Zayd to deliver educational lessons and lectures. However, Shaykh Bakr excused himself because he was occupied with verification [i.e, researching and verifying texts] and writing. Ibn Bāz then said: "His pen suffices us, his pen suffices us."
(End quote from 'Shaykh Bakr Abū Zayd wa ʾĀrāʾuhu at-Tarbawiyyah wal-Ijtimāʿiyyah', p. 86)

This is because Ibn Bāz was a great Imām, a distinguished researcher [Muḥaqqiq], and a man of profound foresight. His concern was for the religion of Allāh to spread, for falsehood to be defeated, and for the truth to triumph through any legitimate means, including beneficial writing. This is the way of the wise and the noble.

As for those to whom this reality is not clear, and who lack a deeper, more comprehensive vision of the ultimate goal of calling to Allāh, they—due to their ignorance and short-sightedness—only see knowledge in the specific domain they have mastered and have been granted success in.

If he is strong in public speaking, he sees daʿwah only through that lens. If he is strong in writing, he belittles those who do not author books or articles, or he belittles preachers and considers them merely speakers rather than people of knowledge. He criticizes and mocks them. If he is strong in teaching, he sees virtue only in those who conduct powerful lessons. If he excels in language, he looks down upon those who do not possess the same mastery.

And so it goes with every field.

Yet it is well known that knowledge is vast. No one has been granted all of it, and no one has had every one of its doors opened for him. Rather, people draw from it according to what Allāh facilitates and provides for them.

This perspective is not unique to our time; rather, it has been inherited from generations past. Look at what Al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn ʿAbd al-Barr (d. 463 AH) recorded:

The worshipful ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz al-ʿUmarī wrote to Mālik, encouraging him to isolate himself and focus solely on personal worship, discouraging him from gathering people for knowledge.

Mālik wrote back to him:
"Indeed, Allāh has distributed actions just as He has distributed provisions. A man may have the door of prayer opened for him, but not fasting. Another may have the door of charity opened, but not fasting. Another may have the door of Jihād opened, but not prayer. And spreading and teaching knowledge is among the greatest righteous deeds, and I am pleased with what Allāh has opened for me in this regard. I do not believe that what I am engaged in is lesser than what you are engaged in, and I hope that both of us are upon goodness and righteousness. It is upon each one of us to be pleased with what has been apportioned to him. Peace be upon you."
[At-Tamhīd limā fil-Muwaṭṭaʾ min al-Maʿānī wal-Asānīd (Vol.7, p.185)]
08:05
Commenting on the previous narration in his biography of al-Imām Mālik, Adh-Dhahabī said:
“How excellent was Mālik’s response to al-ʿUmarī, affirming Allāh’s prior decree concerning His servants. He did not prefer his own path of knowledge over al-ʿUmarī’s path of devotion and asceticism.”
[Tārīkh al-Islām, 11/183]

This is because Mālik’s response was that of a well-grounded Imām who knew exactly what he was saying. He understood Allāh's decrees among His creation, and he recognized the vital importance of every single door of knowledge and goodness. The ultimate goal is to establish Allāh's religion and bring people closer to it.

Thus, you see that the insightful, intelligent person does not confine goodness to a single field, nor does he measure people by his own strengths. Rather, he knows that the doors of righteousness are wide, that Allāh grants His servants whatever goodness He wills, and that people differ in their abilities and aptitudes — all of which are provisions from the Rabb of the earth and the heavens.

So the one for whom the door of knowledge and teaching is opened must not look down on the devout worshipper.
The one for whom the door of jihād is opened must not despise the one devoted to fiqh and issuing fatwas.
The one whom Allāh has blessed with the ability to author books has no right to belittle the sincere preacher, and so on.

The observer of the lives of scholars and rabbāniyyūn finds that each of them performed what was opened for him. So there may be a scholar who was not an orator; an orator who was not a muḥaddith; a muḥaddith who was not a brilliant fiqh scholar. Yet all of them were pillars of the religion and building blocks in daʿwah and guidance, each performing his ordained role without competing with others in what was not opened for him.

Therefore, the words of ʾImām Mālik, the commentary of ʾImām adh-Dhahabī, and the statement of Shaykh Ibn Bāz — are all words of those deeply rooted in knowledge, who recognize the value of oneself and others. They understand that the religion is not upheld by a single man nor a single expertise, but rather by the diversity of generosity and the integration of efforts.

Whoever thinks that goodness only lies in what he himself excels at has narrowed what is vast, has been ignorant, and deluded himself.

The one granted success is he who knows his own worth, proceeds in the door that Allāh has opened and distinguished for him, supports others in the doors of goodness that Allāh has opened for them, rejoices in their success, and does not envy them for the favor of Allāh. The religion is cooperation and completion, not conflict and contempt.
The intelligent one is he whose own good increases through the good of others. The intelligent one is he who sees people as integrated forces working to elevate this religion, not as rivals who push each other aside.

For this reason, some of the Salaf said: "Whoever thinks that no one upholds the truth but him is the most ignorant of people."

For Allāh is the one who divides provisions and brings about goodness through the hands of whomever He wills, whenever He wills, and however He wills. There is no objection to His action, may He be glorified and exalted.

So, O Allāh, open for us and for our brothers the doors of goodness. Make us locks against the doors of evil and the entrances of Shayṭān. Make us all tributaries flowing into the support of Your religion, the guidance of ourselves, their reformation, and whomever You will among Your servants. And all ḥamd is due to Allāh, the Rabb of the worlds.

The Question:
“Assalāmu ʿalaykum wa raḥmatullāhi wa barakātuh. O Shaykh, I ask Allāh that you are in the best of states. I have a question, may Allāh bless you.

In the statement ‘Lā ʾilāha ʾillā Allāh’ (لا إله إلا الله), we know that the word ‘ʾilāh’ in the Arabic language means ‘maʿbūd’ (One who is worshipped). We also know that we derive from the Qurʾān that Tawḥīd is divided into three categories: Tawḥīd al-ʾUlūhiyyah, Tawḥīd ar-Rubūbiyyah, and Tawḥīd al-ʾAsmāʾ wa aṣ-Ṣifāt.

According to what I know, the statement ‘Lā ʾilāha ʾillā Allāh’ in itself encompasses all three categories of Tawḥīd. If this statement is correct, then where is Tawḥīd ar-Rubūbiyyah and Tawḥīd al-ʾAsmāʾ wa aṣ-Ṣifāt within the statement ‘Lā ilāha illā Allāh’? Because this statement mentions ‘Lā ilāha illā Allāh’ meaning ‘There is no diety worthy of worship except Allāh’ — and it does not mention that Allāh, Glorified and Exalted, is One in His Rubūbiyyah and in His Names and Attributes.*

May Allāh reward you, our noble Shaykh, and may Allāh bless you and make your final abode Paradise.”


The Answer:
Wa ʿalaykum as-salām wa raḥmatullāhi wa barakātuh.
The people of knowledge say: Tawḥīd ar-Rubūbiyyah is included in Tawḥīd al-ʾUlūhiyyah, and Tawḥīd al-ʾUlūhiyyah necessarily entails Tawḥīd al-Rubūbiyyah and (Tawḥīd) al-Asmāʾ wa aṣ-Ṣifāt. How is that?

When you affirm and acknowledge that there is no deity worthy of worship except Allāh, you are affirming and acknowledging ʾUlūhiyyah for Allāh, and you are affirming for Him al-ʾAsmāʾ wa aṣ-Ṣifāt.

Is there an ʾilāh who is not a Rabb? Is there an ʾilāh who does not have the Most Beautiful Names and Attributes that indicate His perfection? This can never be.

So if you acknowledge that Allāh is the true ʾilāh, then it is necessary that you acknowledge that He is the Rabb, the Owner, the Disposer, the One who governs the universe.

And if He is the Rabb, the Owner, the Disposer, then it necessarily follows that He is All-Hearing, All-Seeing, All-Knowing, All-Powerful, and other attributes of Allāh.

Thus, the word *Lā ʾilāha ʾillā Allāh* indicates the three categories of Tawḥīd.

- The word ʾilāh means maʿbūd. And the true one worthy of worship cannot be anything other than the Rabb, the Owner, the Disposer, the One who governs, who has the Most Beautiful Names and Lofty Attributes.
- The word maʿbūd is an attribute of Allāh.
- The word Allāh is one of the Names of Allāh from His Beautiful Names.

The attributes of Allāh are divided into two types: affirmed and negated. Both types are present in the statement *Lā ʾilāha ʾillā Allāh*:

- *Lā ʾilāha* — This is negation of any partner, equal, rival, or counterpart to Allāh. Negation of any son or wife, and negation of all attributes of deficiency and imperfection from Allāh.
- *ʾIllā Allāh* — This is affirmation of perfection for Allāh. Thus true ʾUlūhiyyah and absolute perfection is affirmed for Allāh—Glorified and Exalted is He—which contains no deficiency or imperfection in any way whatsoever.

Answered by: The virtuous Shaykh Abū Zayd Muʿāfā ibn ʿAlī al-Maghlāfī.

When distress becomes intense and the hardship grows severe, relief is, in most cases, very near.

Allāh تعالى says:

{حَتَّى إِذَا اسْتَيْأَسَ الرُّسُلُ وَظَنُّوا أَنَّهُمْ قَدْ كُذِبُوا جَاءَهُمْ نَصْرُنَا}
'(They were reprieved) until, when the Messengers gave up hope and thought that they were denied (by their people), then came to them Our Help.' [Yūsuf: 110]

And He said:

{أَمْ حَسِبْتُمْ أَنْ تَدْخُلُوا الْجَنَّةَ وَلَمَّا يَأْتِكُمْ مَثَلُ الَّذِينَ خَلَوْا مِنْ قَبْلِكُمْ مَسَّتْهُمُ الْبَأْسَاءُ وَالضَّرَّاءُ وَزُلْزِلُوا حَتَّى يَقُولَ الرَّسُولُ وَالَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مَعَهُ مَتَى نَصْرُ اللَّهِ أَلَا إِنَّ نَصْرَ اللَّهِ قَرِيبٌ}
'Or think you that you will enter Paradise without such (trials) as came to those who passed away before you? They were afflicted with severe poverty and ailments and were so shaken that even the Messenger and those who believed along with him said, When (will come) the Help of Allāh? Yes! Certainly, the Help of Allāh is near!' [al-Baqarah: 214]

He also informed (us) about Yaʿqūb عليه السلام, that he never despaired of being reunited with Yūsuf, he said to his (Yūsufs) brothers:

{اذْهَبُوا فَتَحَسَّسُوا مِنْ يُوسُفَ وَأَخِيهِ وَلَا تَيْأَسُوا مِنْ رَوْحِ اللَّهِ}
'Go you and enquire about Yūsuf and his brother, and never give up hope of Allāh’s Mercy.' [Yūsuf: 87]

And he said:

{عَسَى اللَّهُ أَنْ يَأْتِيَنِي بِهِمْ جَمِيعًا}
'Maybe Allāh will bring them (back) all to me.' [Yūsuf: 83]

Among the subtle wisdoms behind relief being paired with the height of hardship is that when distress reaches its utmost limit, a person loses hope in the creation's ability to remove it and turns with complete dependence to the Creator alone. When one's heart becomes detached from the creation and fully attached to Allāh, Allāh answers him and removes his hardship.

Indeed, tawakkul means cutting off all hopeful expectation from the creation. This was stated by Imām Aḥmad, who cited as evidence the statement of Ibrāhīm when Jibrīl approached him while he was in the air and asked, "Do you need anything?"
He replied:
"As from you, no."

Tawakkul is among the greatest means by which needs are fulfilled, for Allāh suffices whoever relies upon Him. As He says:

{وَمَنْ يَتَوَكَّلْ عَلَى اللَّهِ فَهُوَ حَسْبُهُ}
'And whosoever puts his trust in Allāh, then He will suffice him.' [aṭ-Ṭalāq: 3]

al-Fuḍayl said: "By Allāh, if you were to despair of the creation to the point that you wanted nothing from them, your Master would give you everything you desire."

Another wisdom is that when a slave is overcome by severe hardship, he must then struggle against Shayṭān, for Shayṭān comes to make him despair and become discontent. The slave must strive against him and repel (his whispers). Thus, the reward for striving against this enemy and overcoming him is that the affliction itself is removed and lifted.

— Nūr al-Iqtibās fī Mishkāt Waṣiyyat an-Nabī ﷺ li-Ibn ʿAbbās, by Ibn Rajab al-Ḥanbalī, p. 173.
